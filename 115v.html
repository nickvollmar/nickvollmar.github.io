xviii. Wiþ wambe wærce 7 rysel wærce þær þu geseo tord wifel on eorþan up weorpan ymbfo hine mid twam handum mid his geweorpe wafa mid þinum handum swite cweþ þriwa: remedium facio ad uentris dolorem . weorp þonne ofer bæc þone wifel on wege beheald þæt þu ne locige æfter þonne monnes wambe wærce oððe rysle ymbfoh mid þinum handum þa wambe him biþ sona sel .xii. monaþ þu meaht swa don æfter þam wifele.
xviii. Against womb pain and abdomen pain, where you see a dung beetle on the ground digging [something] up, grasp it with two hands with his dung ball24. Wave with your hands strongly. Say thrice, "Remedium facio ad ventris dolorem." Then throw that beetle away over the back. Restrain [yourself] so that you do not look after it. When a person's womb or abdomen hurts, grasp the womb with your hands. Soon he is well. Twelve months you may so do after the beetle.
xviiii. Wiþ blædder wærce wudumerce 7 leac cerse wyl swiþe on ealað sele drincan 7 etan gebrædne stær.
xviiii. Against bladder pain, boil wild celery and garlic-mustard vigorously in ale. Give to drink and eat roast starling.
xx. Gif man ne mæge gemigan 7 him weaxan stanas on þære blædran wyl sundcorn on ealað 7 petersilian sele him drincan.
xx. If a person may not urinate and stones grow in his bladder, boil meadow saxifrage and garden parsley in ale. Give him [it] to drink.
xxi. Gif man sie se utgang forseten wyl wermod on surum ealað 7 do buteran þær to him biþ sona sel gif he hit drincþ.
xxi. If a person has his evacuation obstructed, boil wormwood in sour ale and add butter to that. Soon he is well if he drinks it.
xxii. Wiþ utrihtadle .v. leafan hleomoce. curmealle. elehtre. gecnua þa wyrta 7 wyl on meolce sele him drincan wearm on morgenne 7 on æfen;
xxii. Against out-straight disease, [take] five leaves of brooklime, knapweed, lupin. Pound the herbs and boil in milk. Give the warm [drink] to him to drink in the morning and in the evening.
24. Bosworth-Toller translates this passage: "when you see a dung-beetle in the earth throwing up mould, catch it up with both hands along with his casting up."
Geweorp, which BT translates as "casting up," probably refers to the ball of dung/earth which dung beetles create and move around with them. ↩
